Call for Papers

About the symposium

The growing complexity and scope of the changes, challenges, and situations arising from the integration of Generative Artificial Intelligence (Generative AI) into audiovisual and multimodal creation require equally complex theoretical, conceptual, and methodological approaches across different disciplines and fields of research (Ruszvev et al. 2025). Likewise, the functionalities of the new generative models, its multi-level adoption, and the expanding range of uses, establish a paradigm that goes beyond the generation of isolated outputs towards a complete reconfiguration of creative workflows (Santoso & Wijayanti, 2024; Valverde-Valencia, 2025).

The very notion of workflow, when applied to human-AI creativity, requires a distinct approach to a long-established concept within media and communication studies. Traditionally envisioned as a sequential organization to complete any type of work (Oxford UP; Cambridge UP), the concept has been expanded to accommodate the complexity of human-technology relationships within production processes, focusing on the design, coordination and adaptation of work processes through and with technology (Nicoll and Keogh, 2019). The emergence of Generative AI and its integration into creative processes requires a further expansion of this concept to encompass notions such as distributed agency (Celis Bueno et al. 2024) or Human-AI collaboration (Geroimenko, 2025), which frame these processes not as sequential but as dynamic and co-evolutionary (Moruzzi, 2023). Therefore, Human-AI creative workflows can be understood as an entanglement of relationships between actors, practices, and artifacts that includes mutual learning, feedback loops, iteration, strategies and social, ethical and labor implications regarding authorship, meaning, value and impact of both the result and the process. However, this comprehensive perspective on the concept of workflow also raises questions: How is learning organized and performed in these iterative processes? How do the stages of audiovisual creation adapt when Generative AI plays a role in the process? How can the labor implications of these changes be addressed? How do we negotiate value and authenticity when creative agency is distributed?

Considering these questions and the challenges posed by Generative AI in the audiovisual field, this first International Symposium on Human-AI Creative Workflows aims to bring together scholars who are embracing complex approaches to the study of these topics to understand the implications of these workflows at the educational, labor, and creative levels.

We invite proposals that address, but are not limited to the following topics in each of these four thematic sections:

  • Educational approaches: Teaching-learning processes, contexts and strategies in Human-AI Creation and the role of AI Literacy.
  • The monopolization of workflows: Young, Joseph and Nieborg (2025): Workflow monopolies as a process of platformization, infraestructuralization, conglomeration and financialization of concentration of the whole productive and creative pipeline, restricting the relationship with other workflows. Workflow monopolies are likely to be built around emerging technologies such as Generative AI (Young et al. 2025).
  • Agency and Authorship: How is the authorship framed in Human-AI Creativity? Negotiations of control and autonomy in collaborative workflows.
  • Case studies: Case studies on GenAI implementation in studios, newsrooms, and agencies. Resistance, workarounds, and adoption patterns.
